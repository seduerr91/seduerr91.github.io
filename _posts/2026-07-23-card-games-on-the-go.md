# Card Games on the Go With One Deck

A single 54-card deck is one of the best travel toys ever invented.

It fits in a jacket pocket, works at an airport gate, survives a cabin weekend, and can support very different moods: tactical, chaotic, social, quiet, loud, fast, and surprisingly competitive.

These are some of my favorite card games to play on the go with one standard deck. Some of them use the jokers, some ignore them, and one of them works best after trimming the deck down.

## 1. Yaniv

**Players:** 2 to 8, best with 3 to 5

Yaniv, sometimes spelled Yannif, is a popular Israeli rummy-style game. The goal is simple: have the lowest hand total when someone calls "Yaniv!"

## What You Need

- 2 to 8 players, though it is usually best with 3 to 5
- 1 standard 54-card deck including 2 jokers
- These card values:
  - Ace = 1
  - 2 through 10 = face value
  - Jack, Queen, King = 10
  - Joker = 0

Some tables play the Joker as the value of the card it replaces, but `0` is the standard rule.

## Setup

1. Deal 5 cards face down to each player.
2. Turn 1 card face up in the middle to start the discard pile.
3. Place the rest of the deck face down as the draw pile.

## How a Turn Works

Every turn has 2 steps:

1. Discard
2. Draw

### 1. Discard

You must discard, and you have 3 legal options:

- **One card:** Any single card
- **Set:** 2, 3, or 4 cards of the same rank, such as `7 of hearts + 7 of spades + 7 of clubs`
- **Straight:** 3 or more cards in sequence of the same suit, such as `4-5-6 of hearts`

A Joker can be used in the middle of a straight.

### Slapping or Cutting

If someone discards multiple cards, other players can instantly throw matching cards from their own hand on top. This is usually called **slapping** or **cutting**.

For example, if a player discards `7-7` and you have another `7`, you can slap it down immediately without waiting for your turn.

If multiple players try to slap, the first valid slap counts. If you slap by mistake, you take a penalty card.

### 2. Draw

After discarding, draw 1 card:

- Take the top card from the draw pile, or
- Take any card you just discarded

If you discarded a set or a straight, you can only take the **first** or **last** card from that group, not a middle card.

## Calling Yaniv

Instead of taking a normal turn, you can call **"Yaniv!"** if your hand total is **7 or less**. Some tables use **5 or less**.

When someone calls Yaniv, everyone reveals their hand.

- If the caller has the lowest total, they win the round and every other player gets points equal to their hand total.
- If another player has an equal or lower total, the caller gets an **Asaf**: `30 penalty points + their hand total`, and the player with the actual lowest hand gets `0`.

This means calling Yaniv too early can be expensive.

## Scoring the Game

Yaniv is usually played across multiple rounds with a running score.

- The first player to go over `100` or `200` points loses, depending on the table rules.
- The player with the lowest total score wins the full game.

Some common house rules:

- If a player lands on exactly `100` or `200`, their score is cut in half to `50` or `100`.
- In some versions, landing exactly on `50`, `100`, `150`, or `200` gives a `-50` bonus.

## Why the Game Is Fun

Yaniv stays interesting because it mixes three different skills:

- Keeping your hand total low
- Choosing discards that help you without helping everyone else
- Timing your Yaniv call before someone beats you with an Asaf

That is the whole game: low hand, smart discards, and good timing.

## 2. Palace / Shithead

**Players:** 2 to 6

This is one of the best casual travel games because the goal is intuitive: get rid of all your cards before everyone else. It starts simple and then becomes delightfully chaotic.

### Common Setup

In a common version, each player gets:

- 3 face-down cards
- 3 face-up cards placed on top of the face-down cards
- 3 cards in hand

Start a central pile and keep the rest of the deck as a draw pile.

### How It Plays

On your turn, play a card equal to or higher than the top card of the pile. You can usually play multiples of the same rank together.

While the draw pile still exists, players often draw back up to 3 cards after playing.

If you cannot play, you pick up the whole pile.

### Special Cards

House rules vary a lot, which is part of the fun. A common version includes:

- `2` as a reset or wild card
- `10` as a burn card that clears the pile

Some tables add more special rules, but those two are enough to make the game lively.

Once your hand is empty, you play your face-up cards, and then your face-down cards blindly.

## 3. Crazy Eights

**Players:** 2 to 6

Crazy Eights is fast, simple, and especially good with mixed-skill groups or tired travelers.

### Common Setup

Deal 5 cards to each player, or 7 if only 2 people are playing. Turn one card face up to begin the discard pile.

### How It Plays

On your turn, play a card that matches either:

- The rank of the top discard, or
- The suit of the top discard

An `8` is wild. When you play it, you name the suit that the next player must follow.

If you cannot play, draw from the deck. Some groups draw one card, while others draw until a playable card appears.

The first player to empty their hand wins.

## 4. Gin Rummy

**Players:** 2

Gin Rummy is cleaner and more tactical than Yaniv. It is excellent when two people want something a little sharper and more deliberate.

### Common Setup

Deal 10 cards to each player. Turn one card face up to start the discard pile and place the rest face down as the stock.

### How It Plays

Players try to form:

- **Sets:** cards of the same rank
- **Runs:** cards in sequence of the same suit

On your turn:

1. Draw either the top stock card or the top discard
2. Improve your hand
3. Discard one card

The cards not in melds are your **deadwood**.

You can **knock** when your deadwood is low enough, commonly `10` or less. If all your cards are in melds, that is **gin** and is even better.

Gin Rummy rewards memory, hand reading, and good discard discipline.

## 5. Rummy 500

**Players:** 2 to 6

Rummy 500 feels more open and swingy than Gin because the discard pile is much more live and dramatic.

### Common Setup

A common casual setup is 7 cards each. Turn one card face up for the discard pile and leave the rest as the stock.

### How It Plays

Players build sets and runs just like in other rummy games, but scoring is based on the value of cards you successfully lay down.

On your turn, you can:

- Draw from the stock, or
- Take from the discard pile, usually by taking a specific card and the cards above it, as long as you immediately use that card in a meld

Then you may lay down melds, add to existing melds, and discard one card.

The first player to reach `500` points wins.

## 6. Egyptian Rat Screw

**Players:** 2 to 6

This is the loudest game on the list. It is reflex-based, slap-heavy, and very funny when everyone is alert enough to cause trouble.

### Common Setup

Deal the full deck out as evenly as possible. Players keep their cards face down in a personal stack.

### How It Plays

Players take turns flipping one card at a time into the center.

Whenever a face card appears, the next player gets a limited number of chances to respond with another face card:

- Jack: 1 chance
- Queen: 2 chances
- King: 3 chances
- Ace: 4 chances

If they fail, the player who laid the face card wins the pile.

At any time, players can slap the pile on certain patterns. Common slap rules include:

- Doubles
- Sandwiches

First correct slap wins the pile. Wrong slap usually earns a penalty.

Last player with cards wins.

## 7. Durak

**Players:** 2 to 6

Durak is one of the best attack-and-defense card games around. The rules are easy, but the decisions get sharp quickly.

### The Deck

Durak is usually played with a **36-card deck**, so with a standard 54-card deck you normally remove everything below `6` and ignore the jokers.

### Common Setup

Deal 6 cards to each player. Reveal one card to set the trump suit and place the remaining deck beside it.

### How It Plays

The player with the lowest trump usually attacks first.

The attacker plays a card. The defender must beat it with:

- A higher card of the same suit, or
- Any trump card

If the attack succeeds, attackers can add more cards that match ranks already on the table. The defender must beat them all.

If the defender fails, they pick up everything. If they succeed, the cards are discarded.

Players draw back up to 6 cards after each battle.

The goal is to get rid of all your cards. The last player still holding cards is the **Durak**, or fool.

## 8. President / Asshole / Scum

**Players:** 4 to 7

This is a very social climbing game where high cards rule and table politics emerge almost immediately.

### Common Setup

Deal out the entire deck as evenly as possible. Jokers are optional depending on the table.

### How It Plays

The lead player starts with:

- A single card
- A pair
- Or another matching set, depending on the group rules

Each next player must either:

- Play the same number of cards in a higher rank, or
- Pass

When everyone passes, the pile clears and the last player to play successfully leads the next round.

The first player to go out wins the round. The last player out loses it.

In longer sessions, many groups add rank-based privileges for the next round, which is where the President and Scum names come from.

## Which One to Pick

If I wanted a pocket lineup for different situations, I would choose:

- **Yaniv:** low-score rummy tension and smart timing
- **Palace:** casual chaos with very teachable rules
- **Crazy Eights:** the easiest choice for mixed groups
- **Gin Rummy:** the best two-player tactical option
- **Rummy 500:** a more open and dramatic rummy
- **Egyptian Rat Screw:** high-energy slap madness
- **Durak:** attack-defense strategy with a great arc
- **President:** social, competitive, and perfect for a bigger group

One deck really is enough for a whole trip.
